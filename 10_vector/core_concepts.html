Vectors — Core Concepts

1. Scalar Product

\[\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = |\vec{a}||\vec{b}|\cos\theta\]

TipKEY
  • \(\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = \vec{b} \cdot \vec{a}\) (commutative) and \(\vec{a} \cdot (\vec{b} + \vec{c}) = \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} + \vec{a} \cdot \vec{c}\) (distributive)
  • \(\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = 0\) if \(\vec{a}\) and \(\vec{b}\) are perpendicular
  • \(\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = |\vec{a}||\vec{b}|\) if \(\vec{a}\) and \(\vec{b}\) are parallel; in particular, \(\vec{a} \cdot \vec{a} = |\vec{a}|^2\)

Unit vectors \(i, j, k\):

\[i \cdot i = 1, \qquad i \cdot j = 0\]

Column matrix form:

\[\begin{pmatrix}a_1\\a_2\\a_3\end{pmatrix} \cdot \begin{pmatrix}b_1\\b_2\\b_3\end{pmatrix} = a_1b_1 + a_2b_2 + a_3b_3\]

TipKEY — Finding the angle

\[\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = |\vec{a}||\vec{b}|\cos\theta = a_1b_1 + a_2b_2 + a_3b_3\]

\[\cos\theta = \frac{a_1b_1 + a_2b_2 + a_3b_3}{|\vec{a}||\vec{b}|}\]

Ex 1 — Angle between two vectors

Given \(\vec{a} = -i+j+3k\) and \(\vec{b} = 7i-2j+2k\), find the angle between \(\vec{a}\) and \(\vec{b}\).

\[\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = (-1)(7) + (1)(-2) + (3)(2) = -7 - 2 + 6 = -3\]

\[|\vec{a}| = \sqrt{1+1+9} = \sqrt{11}, \qquad |\vec{b}| = \sqrt{49+4+4} = \sqrt{57}\]

\[\cos\theta = \frac{-3}{\sqrt{11}\cdot\sqrt{57}} = \frac{-3}{\sqrt{627}}\]

\[\boxed{\theta = \arccos\!\left(\frac{-3}{\sqrt{627}}\right) \approx 96.9°}\]

Ex 2 — Perpendicular vectors: find \(\lambda\)

Given \(\vec{a} = 2i-6j+k\) and \(\vec{b} = 5i+2j+\lambda k\) are perpendicular, find \(\lambda\).

If perpendicular: \(\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = 0\)

\[(2)(5) + (-6)(2) + (1)(\lambda) = 0\]

\[10 - 12 + \lambda = 0\]

\[\boxed{\lambda = 2}\]

2. Line Equation in 3D

Equation Type 2D 3D
Vector \(\vec{r} = \vec{a} + \lambda\vec{b} = \begin{pmatrix}a_1\\a_2\end{pmatrix} + \lambda\begin{pmatrix}b_1\\b_2\end{pmatrix}\) \(\vec{r} = \begin{pmatrix}a_1\\a_2\\a_3\end{pmatrix} + \lambda\begin{pmatrix}b_1\\b_2\\b_3\end{pmatrix}\)
Parametric \(\begin{cases}x = a_1 + \lambda b_1\\y = a_2 + \lambda b_2\end{cases}\) \(\begin{cases}x = a_1 + \lambda b_1\\y = a_2 + \lambda b_2\\z = a_3 + \lambda b_3\end{cases}\)
Cartesian \(\dfrac{x-a_1}{b_1} = \dfrac{y-a_2}{b_2}\) \(\dfrac{x-a_1}{b_1} = \dfrac{y-a_2}{b_2} = \dfrac{z-a_3}{b_3}\)
NoteNote
  • \(\vec{a}\): position vector of a point on the line
  • \(\vec{b}\): direction vector of the line
  • \(\lambda\): scalar parameter
핵심 아이디어 — 왜 이렇게 쓰나?

직선은 “직선 위의 한 점 + 방향” 만 알면 완전히 결정돼.

\[\vec{r} = \underbrace{\vec{a}}_{\text{직선 위 한 점}} + \lambda\underbrace{\vec{b}}_{\text{방향 벡터}}\]

\(\lambda\)를 바꾸면 직선 위 모든 점을 다 찍을 수 있어. (\(\lambda = 0\)이면 점 \(A\) 자체, \(\lambda = 1\)이면 \(\vec{a}+\vec{b}\), 음수면 반대 방향.)

3가지 표현은 모두 같은 직선이야 — 형태만 다를 뿐:

\[\text{Vector} \xrightarrow{\text{성분별 분리}} \text{Parametric} \xrightarrow{\lambda \text{ 소거}} \text{Cartesian}\]

Vector → Parametric → Cartesian 변환 방법

Step 1. Vector → Parametric (성분별로 분리)

\[\vec{r} = \begin{pmatrix}3\\2\\-5\end{pmatrix} + t\begin{pmatrix}1\\-6\\-2\end{pmatrix} \implies \begin{cases}x = 3+t\\y = 2-6t\\z = -5-2t\end{cases}\]

Step 2. Parametric → Cartesian (각 식에서 \(\lambda\) 구해 연결)

각 식에서 \(t\)를 표현하면: \(t = x-3\), \(t = \dfrac{y-2}{-6}\), \(t = \dfrac{z+5}{-2}\)

모두 같은 \(t\)이므로:

\[\frac{x-3}{1} = \frac{y-2}{-6} = \frac{z+5}{-2}\]

주의: 방향벡터 성분이 \(0\)이면 \(\dfrac{y-1}{0}\) 꼴이 나오는데, 이는 \(y=1\) (그 좌표가 상수)를 의미해. (Ex 6 참고)

Ex 3 — Line through a point, parallel to a vector

Find a vector equation of the straight line which passes through \(A(3,4,4)\) and is parallel to \((7i-4k)\).

\[\boxed{\vec{r} = \begin{pmatrix}3\\4\\4\end{pmatrix} + \lambda\begin{pmatrix}7\\0\\-4\end{pmatrix}}\]

Ex 4 — Line through two points

Find a vector equation of the line through \(A(4,5,-1)\) and \(B(6,3,2)\).

Direction vector: \(\overrightarrow{AB} = B - A = \begin{pmatrix}2\\-2\\3\end{pmatrix}\)

\[\boxed{\vec{r} = \begin{pmatrix}4\\5\\-1\end{pmatrix} + \lambda\begin{pmatrix}2\\-2\\3\end{pmatrix}}\]

Ex 5 — Parametric form from vector equation

Express in parametric form: \(\vec{r} = (3i+2j-5k) + t(i-6j-2k)\)

\[\begin{cases}x = 3 + t\\y = 2 - 6t\\z = -5 - 2t\end{cases}\]

Ex 6 — Cartesian equation from two points

Find a Cartesian equation of the line through \(A(2,1,9)\) and \(B(4,1,-8)\).

Direction vector: \(\overrightarrow{AB} = \begin{pmatrix}2\\0\\-17\end{pmatrix}\)

\[\frac{x-2}{2} = \frac{y-1}{0} = \frac{z-9}{-17}\]

Note: \(\dfrac{y-1}{0}\) means \(y = 1\) (constant — direction component is zero).

3. Types of Questions

Point Line
Point ① distance between two points
Line ② distance from point to line ③ intersection point, ④ angle between lines, ⑤ distance between skew lines (FP)

① Distance between two points

\[d = |\vec{b} - \vec{a}| = \sqrt{(b_1-a_1)^2+(b_2-a_2)^2+(b_3-a_3)^2}\]

③ Intersection of two lines

Set \(l_1 = l_2\) using parameters \(\lambda\) and \(\mu\). Solve the system (2 equations, 2 unknowns). Substitute back to verify the third equation — if inconsistent, lines are skew.

④ Angle between two lines

Use direction vectors \(\vec{b}_1\) and \(\vec{b}_2\):

\[\cos\theta = \frac{|\vec{b}_1 \cdot \vec{b}_2|}{|\vec{b}_1||\vec{b}_2|}\]

Take the absolute value to always get the acute angle.

② Distance from a point to a line (foot of perpendicular)

Let \(F\) be the foot of perpendicular from point \(P\) to line \(l: \vec{r} = \vec{a} + \lambda\vec{b}\).

  1. Write \(F = \vec{a} + \lambda\vec{b}\) (unknown \(\lambda\))
  2. \(\overrightarrow{PF} \cdot \vec{b} = 0\) (perpendicularity condition) → solve for \(\lambda\)
  3. Find \(F\), then \(d = |\overrightarrow{PF}|\)
Ex 7 — Intersection point of two lines

\[l_1: \vec{r} = \begin{pmatrix}3\\8\\-2\end{pmatrix} + \lambda\begin{pmatrix}2\\-1\\3\end{pmatrix}, \qquad l_2: \vec{r} = \begin{pmatrix}7\\4\\3\end{pmatrix} + \mu\begin{pmatrix}2\\1\\4\end{pmatrix}\]

Setting equal: \(3+2\lambda = 7+2\mu,\; 8-\lambda = 4+\mu,\; -2+3\lambda = 3+4\mu\)

From equations 1 and 2: \(\lambda = 2,\; \mu = -1\).

Check equation 3: \(-2+6 = 4\) and \(3-4 = -1\) — not equal, so lines are skew.

Ex 8 — Acute angle between two lines

\[l_1: \vec{r} = \begin{pmatrix}2\\1\\1\end{pmatrix} + \lambda\begin{pmatrix}3\\-8\\-1\end{pmatrix}, \qquad l_2: \vec{r} = \begin{pmatrix}7\\4\\1\end{pmatrix} + \mu\begin{pmatrix}2\\2\\3\end{pmatrix}\]

\[\vec{b}_1 \cdot \vec{b}_2 = (3)(2)+(-8)(2)+(-1)(3) = 6-16-3 = -13\]

\[|\vec{b}_1| = \sqrt{9+64+1} = \sqrt{74}, \qquad |\vec{b}_2| = \sqrt{4+4+9} = \sqrt{17}\]

\[\cos\theta = \frac{|-13|}{\sqrt{74}\cdot\sqrt{17}} = \frac{13}{\sqrt{1258}}\]

\[\boxed{\theta \approx 68.6°}\]

Ex 9 — Distance from point to line

Find the distance from \(P(4,1,-1)\) to \(l: \vec{r} = (4i-4i+3k) + t(i-2j+2k)\).

Let \(F = \begin{pmatrix}4+t\\-4-2t\\3+2t\end{pmatrix}\). Then \(\overrightarrow{PF} = \begin{pmatrix}t\\-5-2t\\4+2t\end{pmatrix}\).

Perpendicularity: \(\overrightarrow{PF} \cdot \begin{pmatrix}1\\-2\\2\end{pmatrix} = 0\)

\[t + 10 + 4t + 8 + 4t = 0 \implies 9t = -18 \implies t = -2\]

\[F = \begin{pmatrix}2\\0\\-1\end{pmatrix}, \qquad \overrightarrow{PF} = \begin{pmatrix}-2\\-1\\0\end{pmatrix}\]

\[\boxed{d = \sqrt{4+1+0} = \sqrt{5}}\]
